9/10

Cross Cultural Differences

A series of experimental studies suggest that people’s intuitions about key philosophical questions might differ from one culture to another. These findings are sometimes thought to undermine certain presuppositions of more traditional philosophical methodology.

Weinberg, J. M., Nichols, S. & Stich, S. Normativity and epistemic intuitions. Philosophical Topics, 29, 429-460.

Seyedsayamdost, H. On normativity and epistemic intuitions: Failure of replication.

Machery, E., Mallon, R., Nichols, S. & Stich, S. Semantics, cross-cultural style. Cognition, 92, B1-B12.

Recommended:

Sytsma, J. & Livengood, J. A new perspective concerning experiments on semantic intuitions. Australasian Journal of Philosophy, 89, 315-332.

9/17

Consciousness

Some entities are seen as having consciousness, others are not. Experimental research in this area looks at people’s ordinary way of distinguishing between the two and then asks whether these intuitive judgments are to be trusted.

Gray, H. M., Gray, K., & Wegner, D. M. (2007). Dimensions of mind perception. Science, 315, 619-619.

Sytsma, J., & Machery, E. (2010). Two conceptions of subjective experience. Philosophical Studies, 151(2), 299-327.

Arico, A., Fiala, B., Goldberg, R. F., & Nichols, S. (2011). The folk psychology of consciousness. Mind & Language, 26, 327-352.

9/24

Knowledge Attributions: Context and Practical Interests

People ordinarily distinguish between things that a person ‘knows’ and things that a person merely ‘believes.’ It has sometimes been suggested that people’s intuitive way of making this distinction depends to some degree on what is at stake. These papers put that claim to an empirical test.

Sripada, C. S. & Stanley, J. (2012). Empirical tests of interest-relative invariantism. Episteme, 9, 3-26.

Buckwalter, W. & Schaffer, J. (2013). Knowledge, stakes, and mistakes. Noûs 47.

Recommended:

DeRose, K. (2011). Contextualism, contrastivism, and x-phi surveys. Philosophical studies, 156(1), 81-110.

10/1

Metaethics

Can moral claims be objectively true, or is morality always merely relative or subjective? These papers examine people’s ordinary intuitions on the topic and use those intuitions to address unresolved issues in metaethics.

Goodwin, G. P., & Darley, J. M. (2010). The perceived objectivity of ethical beliefs: psychological findings and implications for public policy. Review of Philosophy and Psychology, 1, 161-188.

Wright, J. C. & Sarkissian, H. (2013). Folk meta-ethical commitments. In Allhoff, F., Mallon, R. & Nichols, S. (eds.) Philosophy: Traditional & experimental readings. New York: Oxford University Press.

10/8

Introduction to Statistics and Experimental Design

This session will provide a brief lesson in statistics and experimental design, which may be useful both for students who are conducting original experiments and for those who simply want to have a better understanding of the experiments discussed in the course. (Students who are already familiar with experimental methods may want to skip this session.)

10/15

The Impact of Moral Judgment

One might think that people make sense of each other’s actions by first applying certain purely descriptive concepts (causation, intention, etc.) and then making a moral judgment. A series of studies suggest, however, that the situation may be more complex – perhaps people’s moral judgments affect their application even of these seemingly descriptive concepts.

Knobe, J. (2010). Person as scientist, person as moralist. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 33, 315.

Alicke, M. D., Rose, D., & Bloom, D. (2011). Causation, norm violation, and culpable control. Journal of Philosophy, 108, 670.

Sripada, C. S., & Konrath, S. (2011). Telling more than we can know about intentional action. Mind & Language, 26, 353-380.

10/22

Free Will

If everything in our universe is determined, can human beings truly have free will? People appear to be conflicted about this question, with something in their minds drawing them toward the answer ‘yes’ and something drawing them toward the answer ‘no.’ Experimental work in this area seeks to understand what might be pulling us in each of these directions and to get a better sense of which intuitions here are actually warranted.

Nahmias, E., & Murray, D. (2010). Experimental philosophy on free will: An error theory for incompatibilist intuitions. New waves in philosophy of action, 189-215.

Björnsson, G. Incompatibilism and “bypassed” agency

Sommers, T. (2010). Experimental philosophy and free will. Philosophy Compass, 199-212.

Strandberg, C. & Björklund, F. (2013) Is moral internalism supported by folk intuitions?, Philosophical Psychology, 26, 319-335.

11/5

Consequentialism and Deontology

Philosophers have long wondered whether (a) morality is simply a matter of bringing about the best possible outcome or (b) certain acts can be morally wrong even when they lead, on the whole, to the outcome that is best. We will be looking at a series of studies on the cognitive processes that lead people to favor (a) or (b) and at the implications of these studies for moral philosophy.

Greene, J. D. (2007). The secret joke of Kant’s soul. In Sinnott-Armstrong, W. (ed.), Moral Psychology, Vol. 3: The Neuroscience of Morality: Emotion, Disease, and Development. MIT Press.

Woodward, J., & Allman, J. (2007). Moral intuition: Its neural substrates and normative significance. Journal of Physiology-Paris, 101(4), 179-202.

11/12

Metaphysics

Just in the past few months, experimental philosophers have begun turning their attention in a serious way to questions in metaphysics. Here we examine recent experimental studies on intuitions about the nature of objects and intuitions about personal identity.

Rose, D., & Schaffer, J. Folk mereology is teleological.

Scholl, B. J. (2007). Object persistence in philosophy and psychology. Mind & Language, 22(5), 563-591.

Tierney, H., Howard, C., Kumar, V., Kvaran, T., & Nichols, S. (forthcoming). How many of us are there. In J. Sytsma (ed.) Advances in Experimental Philosophy of Mind. Continuum Press.

11/19

Critiques of Experimental Philosophy

Although experimental philosophy has been criticized from a number of different perspectives, perhaps the two most prominent critiques are (a) that experimental philosophers are not looking at the right type of intuitions and (b) that intuitions quite generally are not relevant to philosophy. Here we examine one representative example of each critique.

Kauppinen, A. (2007). The rise and fall of experimental philosophy. Philosophical explorations, 10(2), 95-118.

Cappelen, H. (forthcoming). X-phi without intuitions? In D. Rowbottom & A. Booth (eds.). Intuitions. New York: Oxford University Press.
